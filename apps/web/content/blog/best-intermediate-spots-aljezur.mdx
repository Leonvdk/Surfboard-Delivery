Intermediate here means you paddle out reliably, angle take-offs, and handle shoulder-high waves without panic—past whitewater, not chasing huge Beliche. Near Aljezur, Vale Figueiras, Bordeira, the right side of Arrifana, and Amoreira often fit when conditions align; respect rivermouth currents at Amoreira.

This ranking favours wave quality for progression, repeatability, and manageable hazards — not Instagram prestige or how hard the spot sounds in the car park.

## 1. Vale Figueiras

**What it teaches:** Trimming down long faces, reading sections, and patience in a lineup that spreads along a big beach. You stop fighting for one manic peak and start surfing the wave in front of you.

**Conditions:** West and northwest swell is home territory. Mid tide is often a reasonable default — still verify on site after storms or long flat spells, because sand moves.

**Crowds:** Weekends draw Lisbon and Algarve road-trippers, but the beach is long enough that a five-minute walk often thins the pack.

**Hazards:** Rips on bigger days; long paddle-outs when sets stack; the occasional closeout set that reminds you the Atlantic does not care about your plans.

**Why it ranks first for progression:** Repeatable shoulder-high sessions without needing a reef mindset or a staircase exit. It pairs well with our [best time to surf Aljezur](/blog/best-time-to-surf-aljezur) window planning — autumn swells here can feel like a private gym if you dodge weekends.

## 2. Bordeira

**What it teaches:** Peak selection on a wide canvas, reading wind texture on the water, and commitment on walls that run a little faster than Monte Clérigo on the same forecast.

**Conditions:** Loves W and NW; autumn can be spectacular. Summer can be small — check swell period, not only height.

**Crowds:** August holiday traffic; dawn patrol still wins.

**Hazards:** Rips; closeouts when swell jumps — do not hero the horizon set because your ego wants “one more outside.”

**Why second:** A clear power step up from learner beaches while keeping sand underfoot for most of the lineup. If Bordeira feels like too much, reset at [Monte Clérigo](/blog/monte-clerigo-surf-guide) on a smaller day instead of forcing it.

## 3. Arrifana right (north side of the bay)

**What it teaches:** Long rides when swell wraps, lane discipline next to faster surfers, and how headlands bend energy into organised walls.

**Conditions:** Best on wrapping W and WNW. When the whole bay is big, the character changes — intermediates should know when to surf the centre or sit the session out.

**Crowds:** Quality draws people. Wait your turn, call direction, and do not snake the inside.

**Hazards:** Other surfers; rocks at the south end if you drift wrong; rips through the middle on punchy days.

**Why third:** High ceiling for improvement, but lineup pressure and positioning matter more than at Vale Figueiras. Read the full [Arrifana surf guide](/blog/arrifana-surf-guide) before treating “right” as automatic on every tide.

## 4. Amoreira

**What it teaches:** Rivermouth nuance, shifting banks, and adapting when the ocean moves sand overnight.

**Conditions:** Fun intermediate days happen often — but currents can spike quickly when river flow and swell combine. Mild river days feel playful; pushy river days belong to experienced locals and lifeguards’ judgement.

**Crowds:** Mixed tourists and locals; etiquette counts double because take-off zones can cluster.

**Hazards:** Currents — that is why it ranks fourth despite genuine quality.

**Why fourth:** Fantastic sessions when conditions are mild; demands more ocean IQ than the three spots above. Never surf it on autopilot because yesterday was “fine.”

## Skills to practise at these spots

- Paddle fitness — intermediate surfing is a cardio sport disguised as a hobby.
- Angled take-offs — stop going straight into the pit every time; learn to trim first.
- Lineup awareness — [surf etiquette](/blog/surf-etiquette) is performance, not politeness theatre.

If forecasts still confuse you, [how to read surf conditions](/blog/how-to-read-surf-conditions) closes the gap without magic formulas.

## When not to “progress”

Big winter sets, fatigue, bad sleep, or a hangover are all valid reasons to watch from the cliff. [Surf safety Portugal](/blog/surf-safety-portugal) applies no matter how many trips you have done. Progress is measured in years, not in one heavy session that ends in a trip to the pharmacy.
